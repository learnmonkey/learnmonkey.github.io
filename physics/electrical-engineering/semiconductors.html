What are Semiconductors?

In addition to conductors and insulators, there is also the less common semiconductor. Semiconductors conduct electricity better than insulators, but worse than conductors. Semiconductors are used in diodes and transistors.

Some examples of semiconductors are silicon, gallium arsenide, and germanium. Silicon is the most popular semiconductor, with gallium arsenide second.

How Semiconductors Work

In the conductors and insulators tutorial, we introduced the idea of the valence and conduction bands. In a semiconductor, the valence and conduction bands are close together, but they don't overlap.

Doping

Doping is when we intentionally add impurities to the semiconductor.

Silicon has four valence electrons, so silicon forms a crystal with each silicon atom bonding to four other silicon atoms. Now, what if we add an atom with less valence electrons than silicon, like boron? Boron has three electrons, so there will be a hole with no electron. The electrons will move into the hole, this moving the hole. We often think of the hole as moving. So, this increases the conductance because the electrons are moving. This is called p-type doping.

If we add something like phosphorus to the silicon, we get n-type doping. This is because phosphorus has five valence electrons, so there is one electrons that can move between the atoms.
